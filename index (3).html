Generative Language Models and the Transformation of Scientific Discourse: A Computational Text Analysis

Edlira Gugu · University "Aleksandër Xhuvani," Elbasan, Albania · edlira.gugu@uniel.edu.al

Overview

This paper investigates how generative language models are transforming the linguistic and discursive features of contemporary scientific texts. Using a corpus of 15,154 articles partitioned into pre-LLM (2020–2022) and post-LLM (2023–2024) periods, it documents consistent shifts toward homogenization: reduced lexical diversity, increased semantic similarity, and growing reliance on formulaic argumentative structures. Rather than treating LLMs as neutral tools, the paper argues they function as discursive actors actively shaping scientific communication.

The argument

1
LLMs are not neutral tools. When thousands of researchers use models to draft and polish prose, the models' statistical preferences accumulate into measurable shifts in the linguistic character of scientific literature — without deliberate choice by individual authors.
2
The scale is independently documented. Kobak et al. (2025) found LLM markers in ≥13.5% of 2024 biomedical abstracts. Liang et al. (2025) estimate up to 22% of CS papers are LLM-modified. These are not marginal phenomena.
3
The effects are measurable in the corpus. Lexical diversity declines across all three disciplines. Formulaic phrases increase dramatically ("potentially" +37.5%, "in recent years" +34%). Abstract semantic similarity rises +9.3%. Rhetorical template coverage grows from 32% to 41% of abstracts.
4
Feedback loops amplify the effect. Guo et al. (2024) demonstrate experimentally that training language models on synthetic outputs of predecessors produces consistent further decreases in diversity — the "curious decline of linguistic diversity."
5
The epistemic stakes are real. Messeri & Crockett (2024, Nature) warn of "monocultures of knowing." Doshi & Hauser (2024) show a social dilemma: LLMs improve individual output while reducing collective diversity. Anderson et al. (2024) locate homogenization at the group level.
6
Conclusion for computational text analysis. We must reconsider not only how we analyse texts but what kind of texts we are increasingly analysing. Scientific texts are co-produced through human-algorithm collaboration.

Discussion

1
Are LLMs genuinely discourse agents?
When statistical preferences encoded in training data reproduce in millions of assisted writing sessions, they accumulate into aggregate shifts in the scholarly record. This is structurally different from editorial gatekeeping or disciplinary convention — it is an algorithmic force operating at scale without deliberate authorial choice.
2
Is homogenization harmful?
The evidence suggests a paradox. Doshi & Hauser (2024) demonstrate that LLMs improve individual output while reducing collective diversity. Anderson et al. (2024) locate this at the group level. The decline of "arguably" (−19%) is a concrete instance — a marker of authorial contestation disappearing from the record.
3
What are the epistemic stakes?
Messeri & Crockett (2024, Nature) warn of "monocultures of knowing" — situations in which questions and methods most tractable to computational tools crowd out alternative epistemologies. Sourati et al. (2026) document that LLM-induced homogenization already measurably reduces stylistic variability in real-world scientific writing.
4
What should computational text analysts do?
Reconsider not only how we analyse texts, but what kind of texts we are increasingly analysing. Interpretive validity, historical comparability, and methodological transparency all require fresh consideration when texts are co-produced through human-algorithm collaboration.
